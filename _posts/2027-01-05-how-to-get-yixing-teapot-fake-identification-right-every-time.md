## What You Will Need

Before starting, gather these essentials:

- **Phoenix Dan Cong tea leaves** — about 5 grams for a 100ml gaiwan

- **Soft spring water** — filtered or spring, never distilled

- **A gaiwan or Yixing teapot** — 80-120ml is ideal

- **A fairness cup (gongdao bei)** — to ensure even pours

- **Small tasting cups** — porcelain, around 30-50ml

- **A kettle with temperature control** — you need 95°C, not boiling

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Warm Your Vessels

Pour hot water into your gaiwan, fairness cup, and tasting cups. Let it sit for 30 seconds, then discard. Warm vessels keep the tea at the right temperature during brewing — this is not optional, it is essential.

### Step 2: Measure Your Leaves

Use about 5 grams of Dan Cong for every 100ml of water. This is a higher leaf-to-water ratio than most people expect, but Dan Cong needs it. The leaves are tightly rolled and need space to unfurl completely.

### Step 3: Rinse The Leaves

Pour hot water over the leaves and immediately discard — this is called the awakening rinse. It takes about 3 seconds. This step cleans the leaves and opens them up, but it is not a real brew. Do not drink it.

### Step 4: The First Infusion

Pour water at 95°C over the leaves and steep for exactly 5 seconds. Yes, 5 seconds. The first infusion should be quick — it captures the top notes, the most delicate aromas that vanish if you wait too long. Pour into the fairness cup, then into tasting cups.

### Step 5: Continue Steeping

For each subsequent infusion, add 5 seconds. By the fifth infusion, you should be steeping for about 25 seconds. A good Dan Cong will give you 8-12 infusions — the flavor profile changes each time, and that is the point.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

Even experienced tea drinkers get Dan Cong wrong. Here are the most common errors:

- **Using boiling water** — this scorches the leaves and destroys the aroma

- **Steeping too long** — anything over 10 seconds for the first infusion will taste bitter

- **Using too few leaves** — Dan Cong needs a high ratio to fully develop

- **Skipping the rinse** — the first rinse prepares the leaves and removes dust

- **Using hard tap water** — minerals mask the delicate fragrance

## Tasting Notes: What To Expect

A properly brewed Phoenix Dan Cong unfolds in stages. The first sip is floral and bright, almost perfume-like. The second infusion brings out fruit and honey notes. By the fifth infusion, you taste the mineral depth of the mountain itself. The fragrance lingers in the empty cup long after the tea is gone — this is called the cup fragrance, and it is one of the great pleasures of Dan Cong.

The Gongfu tea tradition that developed in Chaozhou was not just about making tea — it was about slowing down, paying attention, and finding beauty in small details.
